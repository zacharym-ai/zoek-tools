Starting From Zero

You just started.
Here's what actually matters first.

Most marketing advice assumes you already have a website, a Google listing, and some customers. If you're brand new — just got your LLC, just picked your business name, just started taking customers — this is your starting point.

Don't run ads yet. Don't worry about social media yet. Here's the real order of operations.

6
Things to do first before spending a single dollar on marketing. Most new businesses skip straight to ads and waste their budget.
82%
Of small businesses fail within 5 years — most not because of the product, but because nobody could find them or trust them online.
The order of operations — do these in order
1
Get your Google Business Profile FREE
This is the single highest-ROI thing a new local business can do. Go to google.com/business and claim your listing. Fill out every field — hours, photos, description, services. This is how you show up when someone searches "plumber near me" or "[your service] in [your city]." Without this, you're invisible.
⏱ Takes 30 minutes 💰 Free 🏆 Highest impact first step
2
Get a website — even a simple one
You don't need anything fancy to start. You need: your business name, what you do, where you serve, how to contact you, and a few photos of your work. That's it. A $15/month Wix site is infinitely better than no website. 73% of customers check a website before calling. If you don't have one, you're losing those people to whoever does.
⏱ Takes a weekend 💰 $15–50/mo DIY or hire Zoek
3
Ask your first customers for a Google review — immediately
Right after you finish a job, send a text: "Hey [name], thanks so much — if you have 2 minutes, a Google review would mean the world: [link]." Get your review link from your GBP dashboard. Your first 10 reviews are the hardest and the most important. Businesses with 10+ reviews get 3× more clicks than those with fewer.
⏱ 2 min per customer 💰 Free
4
Get listed in the top directories
Yelp, Facebook Business, Apple Maps, Bing Places, and your industry-specific directories. These are called citations and they tell Google your business is real. Make sure your name, address, and phone number are exactly the same on every listing — even small differences hurt your rankings.
⏱ 2–3 hours one time 💰 Free (or Zoek Get Listed)
5
Pick ONE social platform and post consistently
Not three. Not five. One. Pick where your customers actually are — Facebook for most local service businesses, Instagram if your work is visual. Post 3× a week: show your work, introduce yourself, share a tip. Consistency beats quality at this stage. A blurry photo of a finished job posted every week beats a polished graphic posted once a month.
⏱ 30 min/week 💰 Free
6
Now consider paid marketing — only after steps 1–5
Ads send people to your website and Google listing. If those aren't ready, ads just waste money. Once you have a solid GBP, a working website, and a handful of reviews — that's when paid traffic starts converting. Best first paid option for most local businesses: Google Local Service Ads (pay per lead, not per click).
⚠ Only after steps 1–5 💰 $300–$1,000/mo to start
Mistakes most new businesses make
✗ What not to do first
Running ads before you have a website. You're paying to send people somewhere that doesn't convert. Every click is wasted money.
Trying to be on every social platform. Five mediocre accounts is worse than one great one. You'll burn out and abandon all of them.
Waiting until everything is perfect. An imperfect website live today gets found. A perfect website launching "next month" doesn't exist yet.
Ignoring your Google Business Profile. Most new owners claim it and never update it. Incomplete profiles rank below competitors who took 30 extra minutes.
✓ What actually works
Tell everyone you know. Your first 10 customers almost always come from your personal network. Don't be shy about it.
Document everything you do. Take photos of every job, every product, every result. This becomes your content library for months.
Ask for referrals directly. "Do you know anyone else who could use this?" is the most powerful marketing question a new business can ask.
Answer your phone. New businesses lose leads constantly by not picking up. If you can't always answer, set up a professional voicemail and call back within the hour.
📋 Your first 30 days
Week 1: Claim Google Business Profile, set up basic website, get your review link ready.
Week 2: Tell your network, get your first 3–5 customers, ask each one for a Google review.
Week 3: Submit to Yelp, Facebook, Apple Maps, and 2–3 industry directories.
Week 4: Pick one social platform, post your first 5 pieces of content. You now have a foundation.

Email Marketing 101

Email is not dead.
You just have to use it right.

Most small businesses either have no email strategy or blast the same message to everyone once a month hoping something sticks. Neither works.

The businesses that win with email treat it like a conversation, not a megaphone. The average ROI for email is $36 for every $1 spent. You own the list. No algorithm. No platform changing the rules overnight.

$36
Average return for every $1 spent on email. Higher than social, paid search, and display combined.
4B+
People use email every day. More than any social platform. Your customers are in there.
3×
More likely to purchase from email than from social media. Higher intent, higher conversion.
Calculate your email ROI

Before building an email strategy, it helps to know what it's actually worth. Plug in your numbers and see what a healthy email list means for your bottom line.

The rule of thumb: if your list isn't generating at least $1 per subscriber per month, your email marketing has room to grow. Most businesses with a working strategy hit $2–5 per subscriber.

The single biggest lever — segmentation

The number one mistake small businesses make with email is treating their list like one group of people. It is not. A lead who found you on Google is different from a referral. Someone who bought once is different from a long-term client.

When you send the same email to all of them, you are relevant to none of them. Segmentation is splitting your list into groups so each group gets a message that actually applies to them. A 10% increase in relevance can double your open rate.

By behavior
Opened last 3 emails
Your most engaged subscribers. Send them your best offer. They're already warm.
By status
Past clients
They've paid you before. Different message than cold leads. Focus on upsell and referral asks.
By source
Google vs referral
Referrals trust you more from the start. Google leads need more nurturing before they convert.
By inactivity
No open in 90 days
Win-back sequence. One last good offer. If no response, remove them — clean lists perform better.

Local SEO 101

97% of people search
for local businesses online.

Local SEO is the process of making sure your business shows up when people in your area search for what you sell. It's different from regular SEO because proximity, relevance, and prominence all factor in — not just keywords.

The good news: most of your local competitors are doing this wrong. A business that gets the basics right consistently will dominate local search within 3–6 months.

76%
Of people who search for something nearby on their phone visit a business within a day.
28%
Of local searches result in a purchase within 24 hours. The intent is immediate.
46%
Of all Google searches have local intent. Nearly half of everyone searching Google wants something near them.
The 3 things Google looks at for local results
📍
Proximity
How physically close your business is to the searcher. You can't fake proximity — but you can optimize the radius by being precise about your service area in Google Business Profile and on your website.
🎯
Relevance
How well your business matches what someone searched for. This comes from your GBP category, your website copy, and the keywords in your reviews. The more specific you are, the better you rank for specific searches.
⭐
Prominence
How well-known and trusted Google thinks you are. Determined by review count, review recency, response rate, backlinks, citations, and time in business. This is the one you have the most control over.
Local ranking signals — what matters most
Google Business Profile completeness
25%
Category, hours, photos, attributes, Q&A, posts. Most businesses are only 40% complete.
Reviews (count + quality + recency)
20%
Getting 1 new review per week consistently beats competitors with more reviews but none recently.
On-page SEO (city + service pages)
15%
"Plumber in Cleveland" on your homepage title and H1. Sounds obvious. Most businesses skip it.
Citations (NAP consistency)
11%
Name, Address, Phone identical across Yelp, YP, BBB, Apple Maps. One wrong digit hurts you.
Behavioral signals (clicks, calls, directions)
10%
When people click your GBP listing and call or get directions, Google takes that as a quality signal.
Backlinks (local relevance matters)
8%
Local newspaper mentions, chamber of commerce links, sponsor pages. Local backlinks beat generic ones.
The 5 things to fix this week
1. Claim and complete your GBP
If you haven't claimed your Google Business Profile, do it today. Add every photo, every category, every attribute. Fill in the description with your city and your top 3 services.
2. Ask for reviews — every time
Build it into your process. After every job, every appointment, every sale — ask. A direct link to your Google review page removes all friction. Respond to every single review.
3. Add city + service to your homepage
Your <title> tag and H1 should say "[Service] in [City]." If your homepage says "Welcome to our website" you are invisible. Rewrite it today.
4. Fix your citations
Search for your business on Yelp, YP.com, Apple Maps, and BBB. Make sure your Name, Address, and Phone are identical everywhere. One discrepancy hurts you.
How PPC Works

You pay when
someone clicks your ad.

Pay-per-click advertising means you only pay when someone actually clicks on your ad — not when they see it. No impressions fee. No minimum spend per view. You're buying intent, not eyeballs.

When done right, PPC is the fastest way to put your business in front of people who are actively searching for exactly what you sell right now. The math is simple: spend less per lead than a lead is worth to you.

Common myths vs reality
MYTH
"PPC is too expensive for small businesses." Reality: PPC is expensive when your landing page is bad or your targeting is off. With the right setup, a $500/mo budget can generate a positive ROI within 60 days.
REALITY

AI & GEO Visibility

Search is changing.
Here's what it means for you.

Google, ChatGPT, Perplexity, and other AI tools are increasingly answering questions directly — without sending users to a website. This is called Generative Engine Optimization (GEO).

For local businesses, this is both a threat and an opportunity. Businesses that get cited by AI systems will see a new stream of high-intent customers. Businesses that ignore it will slowly become invisible.

60%
Of Google searches now end without a click — answered by AI Overviews directly in the results page.
2026
Analysts project AI search engines will handle over 30% of all search queries within 18 months.
How AI decides what to recommend
📝
Authoritative content
AI systems pull from pages that clearly answer specific questions. FAQ pages, detailed service descriptions, and blog posts that answer "how" and "what" questions are prime targets.
⭐
Reviews and reputation signals
When someone asks an AI "who is the best plumber in Cleveland," it reads reviews across Google, Yelp, and other platforms. High ratings + high volume = more AI citations.
🔗
Citations and mentions
If other credible websites — news outlets, directories, local blogs — mention your business, AI systems treat that as a trust signal. It's the same logic as traditional SEO, amplified.
📍
Structured data and schema
Schema markup on your website tells AI crawlers exactly what your business does, where you are, and what you offer. Most local businesses have none. This is a competitive opening.
Traditional SEO vs GEO — what changes
Traditional SEO
Optimize for ranking position 1–10

Page speed and technical signals

Optimize to be cited in AI answers

Social Media 101

Social media that
actually does something.

Most small businesses treat social media like a bulletin board — post something occasionally, hope someone sees it, wonder why nothing happens. That's not social media. That's shouting into a void.

Social media works when it's consistent, intentional, and matched to the right platform. Here's how it actually works.

5B+
People use social media globally. Your customers are on at least one platform — the question is which one and what they want to see.
3×
Businesses that post consistently see 3× more engagement than those who post sporadically — even if the sporadic posts are higher quality.
78%
Of consumers are more likely to buy from a brand they follow on social media. Presence builds trust over time.
Platform guide — where your business actually belongs
📘
Facebook
Best for: Home services, healthcare, restaurants, real estate, professional services — any local business with a 35+ audience.

Facebook's reach has declined for organic posts, but its ad targeting is still the most powerful in local marketing. Paid beats organic here. If you're only going to run ads on one platform, this is usually it.
📸
Instagram
Best for: Beauty, fitness, restaurants, retail, real estate — any business where the work is visual.

Instagram rewards consistency and aesthetics. Reels get the most organic reach right now. Before/after content, behind-the-scenes, and customer results outperform promotional posts every time.
🎵
TikTok
Best for: Restaurants, fitness, beauty, retail, any business that can show the work on video.

The only platform where a brand new account can reach thousands of people organically. Short-form video showing real work, real people, real results. Authenticity beats production value.
💼
LinkedIn
Best for: Professional services, B2B, contractors, consultants — anyone selling to other businesses or professionals.

Organic reach is still strong for personal profiles. Share expertise, not promotions. One thoughtful post per week beats daily promotional content by a wide margin.
▶️
YouTube
Best for: Any business that can explain or demonstrate what they do — contractors, lawyers, healthcare, fitness.

YouTube videos rank in Google search. A 3-minute video answering "how much does a kitchen remodel cost?" can generate leads for years. The highest ROI content you can create — but the highest effort too.
❓
The honest answer
Don't be on every platform. Pick the 1–2 where your customers actually spend time and do those well. A business that posts consistently on Facebook and Instagram will outperform one trying to juggle 5 platforms with no strategy.
The content that actually gets engagement
✓ What works
Before & after — show the transformation. A messy yard → pristine landscaping. Chipped nails → perfect set. Broken pipe → clean repair. The most shareable content format for service businesses.
Behind the scenes — show the work. People are fascinated by how things get made or done. A 30-second Reel of a job in progress gets more reach than a promotional graphic.
Customer wins — share a testimonial with the customer's photo (with permission), or a screenshot of a 5-star review. Social proof in a social feed is extremely powerful.
Educational tips — "3 signs your HVAC needs service before summer." Useful content positions you as the expert and gets saved and shared.
✗ What doesn't
Promotional graphics — "Call us for a free estimate!" posted to a stock photo background. Nobody engages with this. It looks like an ad, gets treated like an ad, and gets ignored.
Inconsistent posting — 5 posts one week, nothing for 3 weeks. The algorithm punishes dormant accounts and audiences forget you exist. Boring and consistent beats brilliant and sporadic.
Irrelevant shares — sharing motivational quotes or viral memes that have nothing to do with your business. It fills the calendar but builds no brand equity.
Ignoring comments — posting and ghosting. Not responding to comments signals you're not actually engaged. The algorithm also rewards accounts that spark conversation.
📐 The framework
The 3-1 rule: For every 3 posts that educate, entertain, or show your work — post 1 that promotes. Most businesses do this backwards and wonder why nobody cares.
Batch your content: Set aside 2 hours once a month and create 8–12 posts at once. Schedule them out. This is more sustainable than trying to post something every day.
Video first: On every platform right now, video content gets 2–3× the organic reach of static images. Even a 15-second clip filmed on your phone outperforms a polished graphic.
Respond within 24 hours: Comments, DMs, and reviews. Responsiveness is a trust signal — 78% of customers expect a reply within a day.
